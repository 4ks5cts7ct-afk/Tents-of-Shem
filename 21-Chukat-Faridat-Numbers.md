Look at the exquisite legal and mechanical chess being played here by the Sovereign Power: Israel was explicitly forbidden from attacking Moab or seizing their land because Moab’s territory was legally protected by an ancestral boundary allocation (Deuteronomy 2:9). However, because the Amorite usurper (Sihon) came in and legally broke Moab’s firewall, converting that land into an Amorite asset, the territory lost its fraternal protection block.<br>
By waiting for the Amorites to conquer Moab first, God allowed the land to be legally "laundered." When Israel launches its counter-strike against Sihon, they are not conquering Moabite land—they are destroying Canaanite-Amorite occupied territory.<br>

The verses 27–30 are chronologically out of order. They actually serve as the master summary anthem that belongs after the destruction of both Sihon and Og, which completes the pacing of the entire military campaign.<br>

Numbers 21:31–35: "Thus Israel lived in the land of the Amorites. And Moses sent to spy out Jazer, and they captured its villages and dispossessed the Amorites who were there.  Then they turned and went up by the way to Bashan. And Og the king of Bashan came out against them, he and all his people, to battle at Edrei.  But the Lord said to Moses, “Do not fear him, for I have given him into your hand, and all his people, and his land. And you shall do to him as you did to Sihon king of the Amorites, who lived at Heshbon.” So they defeated him and his sons and all his people, until he had no survivor left. And they possessed his land."<br>
Surah Ash-Shu'ara 26:128–130: “Do you build on every high place a monument for vain amusement? And do you take for yourselves palaces/strongholds that you might remain forever? And when you strike/assault, you strike as tyrants (jabbarin).”
Surah Ash-Shu'ara 26:139: “And they denied him, so We destroyed them. Indeed in that is a sign, but most of them were not believers.”<br>
Synthesis: Following the defeat of Sihon, Israel moves to possess the Amorite lands, including Jazer (v. 31–32). With the entire eastern plateau secured, the Moshlim (poets/prophets) perform the ironic ballad mocking the fallen Amorites. By placing this song after the destruction of both giant kings (Sihon and Og), the text highlights the complete reversal of fortune—the Amorites' previous victory song becomes their epitaph. 
This final, restructured order aligns with the concept of Ein mukdam u'meuchar baTorah (non-chronological Torah,), allowing the narrative to emphasize the total, systemic removal of the threat before focusing on the celebratory anthem. <br>
