### Baal of Peor (Ba'al Pe'or)<br>

#### The Etymology: Ba'al means "lord," "master," or "husband". Pe'or comes from the Hebrew root pa'ar, meaning "to open wide" or "gape".<br>

#### The Location: Its physical origin was Mount Peor, a high volcanic peak located in the Transjordan region of Moab, overlooking the very plains where Israel was camped. The site is located near Mount Nebo in modern-day Jordan.<br>
 It literally translates to "The Lord of the Wide Opening" or "The Lord of the Chasm."<br>

#### The Underworld Connection (The Chasm)<br>
In Ugaritic and Canaanite mythology, Baal was the god of rain, agriculture, and fertility. The ancient texts reveal that during the dry season, Baal was believed to be swallowed by Mot (the god of death) into the netherworld. He would enter through a "hole" or "chasm" in the earth. Pagan worshippers at Peor would offer sacrifices into holes or fissures in the mountain rocks. As Psalm 106:28 records, this cult was explicitly tied to "eating sacrifices offered to the dead." <br>

#### The Excremental Rites (The Talmudic Record)<br>
Because the root Pe'or means "to open wide" or "uncover," the ancient Near-Eastern cultic practice involved intentional, ritualized degradation. The Babylonian Talmud (Sanhedrin 60b) and Rashi document a shocking reality: the primary method of worshiping Baal of Peor was defecating in front of the idol. Worshippers would expose their rectums—the ultimate anatomical symbol of impurity and waste—directly onto the sacrificial altar. It was a ritualized celebration of total exposure, waste, and bodily release.<br>

As a fertility cult, it heavily integrated ritual orgies and sacred prostitution. Pagan priests and local Midianite/Moabite women would copulate with the worshippers at the shrines. This was meant to magically stimulate Baal to send rain for their harvests.<br>
