The original text of Isaiah 21:13–14 establishes what the prophet actually said: “The burden against Arabia: In the thickets of Arabia you must lodge, O caravans of Dedanites. Bring water for the thirsty, O dwellers of the land of Tema; meet the refugees with food.” The Plain Meaning (Peshat): Isaiah is actually issuing a command of hospitality to the Arabian tribes (Tema and Dedan, who are descendants of Ishmael). He sees a vision of refugees fleeing a brutal war. He calls upon the local desert dwellers to act righteously—to go out into the heat, intercept the desperate refugees, and save them with bread and water. In its original context, Isaiah is praising or instructing these specific Arab tribes to be a source of mercy in a time of regional chaos. Recognizing this literary inversion exposes a timeless geopolitical truth: the existence of bad actors within a group must never be used to indict an entire population. The Sages utilized the tragedy of isolated, historical desert betrayals to process their overwhelming grief, transforming Isaiah's call for mercy into a lament over fractured alliances.
