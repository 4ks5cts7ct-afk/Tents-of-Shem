The synthesis of Jeremiah 32:6–27 with Parsha Behar (Leviticus 25:1–26:2) centers on the intersection of divine law and radical faith regarding the land of Israel. While Behar provides the legal blueprints for land ownership, Jeremiah 32 provides the ultimate "field test" of those laws under the worst possible circumstances. 
1. The Legal Duty: Geulah (Redemption)
The Law (Behar): Leviticus 25 details the laws of Geulah (redemption), which mandate that if a kinsman falls into poverty and must sell his land, his closest relative has the first right and duty to "redeem" it to keep it within the family heritage. 
The Action (Jeremiah): Jeremiah 32 is a literal fulfillment of this law. When his cousin Hanamel offers him a field in Anathoth, Jeremiah exercises his right of redemption as the "next of kin" (go'el). 
2. The Logic of Faith vs. The Logic of War
Behar’s Challenge: The Parsha anticipates human anxiety, asking, "What shall we eat in the seventh year?". God’s answer is to trust in His blessing despite the apparent lack of resources. 
Jeremiah’s Challenge: Jeremiah is commanded to buy land while the Babylonian army is literally at the gates. Logically, the purchase is absurd—the city is about to fall and the land will be worthless Babylonian territory. Jeremiah’s obedience mirrors the "irrational" trust required to observe the Sabbatical (Shmita) year in Behar. 
3. The Consequences of "Exiling" the Land
The Broken Covenant: The sages teach that the 70-year Babylonian exile mentioned in Jeremiah was specifically a punishment for Israel failing to observe 70 Shmita (Sabbatical) years as commanded in Parsha Behar. 
The Divine Owner: Both passages emphasize that the land ultimately belongs to God, not man. In Behar, this means the land must rest; in Jeremiah, it means that even if the land is conquered, God can and will restore it because He is the "God of all flesh". 
4. A Guarantee of Return
Eternal Possession: The act of signing the deed and storing it in an earthenware vessel (Jeremiah 32:14) is a symbolic protest against hopelessness. It serves as a physical guarantee that, just as the Jubilee year in Behar returns all land to its original owners, God will eventually return His people to their ancestral property.
